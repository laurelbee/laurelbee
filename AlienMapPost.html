Using Spatial Analysis Techniques for Alien Abduction Risk Mapping

Spatial analysis techniques are essential is GIS work, as they can lay the foundation for projects in many disciplines including city planning, risk management, business development and so on. In spring of 2024 I created this project as part of my coursework in a class about the environmental applications of GIS with the goal applying spatial analysis techniques that I have learned to a lighthearted and unconventional project.

I felt inspired by theoretical projects that were discussed in different GIS classes that I have taken. One project idea that I heard about was a suitability analysis to determine the best places to release a deadly mycotoxin in order to take out as much of the human population as possible. This was obviously inspired by the television show "The Last Of Us." Another project idea was a risk analysis to determine areas that could be at risk during a zombie apocalyplse. After doing some light research to determine a topic, I decided to do a hazard risk analysis for extraterrestrial contact after coming across the National UFO Reporting Center Website which showcases a map that includes information about self-reported UFO sightings. I was intrigued had to learn more.

The purpose of the analysis that I performed was to determine areas within the state of Pennsylvania that could be at risk of an alien contact scenario. Scholarly articles and popular cultural phenomena were referenced in order to choose appropriate constraints for the analysis. Factors that were taken into consideration included proximity to historically recorded UFO sightings, mental health status, land use, and proximity to military base locations.

While the existence of intelligent extraterrestrial life remains unconfirmed, this project shows how consideration of anecdotal evidence and geographic information along with spatial analysis techniques were able to be used to perform a complete analysis. Although the project is meant to be more lighthearted, a project like this can serve as a valuable foundation for future researchers, GIS analysts , and emergency management professionals by demonstrating the applications of spatial analysis techniques in risk mapping. I would go as far to say that exploring playful or unconventional ideas in a project like this can showcase the potential for creative problem-solving and interdisciplinary collaboration. The model produced from the study can act as a template for similar studies. If nothing else, perhaps the project can provide inspiration for other aspiring GIS professionals.
